Projects

Dynamic myocardial blood volume mapping with MR Multitasking

In my current postdoctoral work at UCLA, I am developing a dynamic framework for fractional myocardial blood volume (fMBV) mapping using an MR Multitasking sequence and ferumoxytol as an intravascular contrast agent. Multitasking acquires highly undersampled data and reconstructs a low-rank latent subspace that resolves cardiac phase, respiratory motion, and contrast dynamics simultaneously.

My goal is to obtain motion-resolved T1 maps across the cardiac cycle and multiple ferumoxytol doses, then fit a biophysical model to derive dynamic fMBV maps.

Contrast-free multi-quantitative MRI with MR Fingerprinting (PhD project)

My PhD work focused on the development of new MR Fingerprinting (MRF) methods for fast multi-parametric and contrast-free quantitative imaging.

The core idea was to directly match in vivo MRF signals to a large dictionary of numerical simulations that incorporate realistic NMR physics (field inhomogeneity, susceptibility, diffusion, relaxation) and biophysical features (cellular and vascular microstructure, flow). I worked on:

  • Designing efficient MRF acquisition schemes for multi-parametric brain imaging.
  • Building realistic digital phantoms of brain microvascular tissue.
  • Accelerating large-scale MRF simulations and dictionary compression with AI tools.
  • Extracting relaxation, perfusion, and oxygenation parameters without contrast injection.

This framework targets quantitative biomarkers for acute stroke and other neurovascular diseases by linking the MRF signal evolution to underlying tissue microstructure and hemodynamics.

Fast MR signal simulations of microvascular and diffusion contributions using histogram-based approximation and recurrent neural networks

In parallel with my MRF work, I developed the MR-WAVES framework, which uses recurrent neural networks (RNNs) to predict MR signals from realistic 3D microvascular geometries incorporating water-diffusion modelling. Classical Monte Carlo simulations of spin dynamics in complex vasculature are accurate but computationally expensive, limiting their use for large-scale sequence optimization or parameter fitting.

MR-WAVES addresses this by:

  • Generating realistic microvascular networks and associated biophysical parameters.
  • Simulating reference MR signals under various gradient waveforms and sequence settings.
  • Training RNN-based surrogate models to learn the temporal evolution of the MR signal.
  • Enabling fast prediction of MR signals for new sequence designs and microvascular scenarios.

This approach makes it possible to explore large spaces of sequence parameters and tissue properties, and to link microvascular architecture to measurable MR contrasts in a computationally tractable way.
